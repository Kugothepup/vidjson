ðŸ“· Camera Lens Types

Wide Angle (14-24mm)

Great for landscapes, architecture, and establishing shots. Creates dramatic perspective and includes more of the scene.

Standard (35-50mm)

Natural perspective similar to human vision. Perfect for general purpose shooting and realistic scenes.

Portrait (85-135mm)

Ideal for close-ups and character focus. Creates natural compression and pleasing bokeh.

Telephoto (200mm+)

Strong compression effect, isolates subjects from background. Great for dramatic effect and distant subjects.

Macro

Extreme close-up capability for tiny subjects. Creates intimate, detailed perspectives.

Fisheye

Ultra-wide with distinctive curved distortion. Creates unique, immersive perspectives.

ðŸŽ¬ Camera Angles & Movements

Eye Level

Natural, neutral perspective at subject's eye height. Most common and comfortable viewing angle.

Low Angle

Camera below subject looking up. Makes subjects appear powerful, heroic, or imposing.

High Angle

Camera above subject looking down. Makes subjects appear vulnerable or diminished.

Bird's Eye

Directly overhead view. Great for showing layout, patterns, or creating unique perspective.

Dutch Angle

Tilted camera creating diagonal horizon. Adds tension, unease, or dynamic energy.

Tracking Shot

Camera follows subject's movement. Creates smooth, cinematic motion. Use Steadicam for stability.

Dolly Shot

Camera physically moves toward/away from subject. Creates dramatic depth and scale changes.

Crane Shot

Sweeping vertical movement. Reveals scale and creates epic, cinematic moments.

Handheld

Natural, documentary-style movement. Adds intimacy and realism to scenes.

Dolly Zoom (Vertigo Effect)

Dolly in while zooming out (or vice versa). Creates disorienting psychological effect.

Pan/Tilt

Horizontal (pan) or vertical (tilt) camera rotation. Reveals environment or follows action.

Static Shot

No camera movement. Focuses attention on subject performance and composition.
